A (Brief) Introduction to Objective-C and the Iterative Nature of Design

The Idea

While out to dinner several times during Summer 2014, I thought a lot about how annoying splitting checks among friends can be. I had just started using Venmo and saw an opportunity to make the process of splitting a check way easier. My friend and roommate at the time Gary and I decided to try our hand at learning Objective-C and building out our idea.

Iterative Design

It needed to be fast, simple and clutter free. Early drawings of Split! introduced me to some of the challenges that come along with mobile: lworking with very little screen real estate and while needing to communicate a lot of information, reduced context, relying on gestures and a plethora of other considerations.

an image goes here :^)

Learning Objective-C was a cool experience, and I wanted to make sure that all of the interactions were smooth and made sense before putting effort into the aesthetic. Eventually my head was moving faster than I could code, and I started putting together mocks using Sketch and InVision so I could more quickly iterate and figure out how to build the best experience for Split!

another image goes here :^^)

After several iterations, I felt that I had designed an experience that let users effortlessly break down checks, split them up and clearly understand all of the information associated with the participants and payments in each transaction. I designed support for OCR and Venmo integration and was very happy with the finished prototype -- it felt like a product that could offer a delightful experience when I needed it, serve it's purpose and be out of the way in a flash.

Takeaways

Although we never really put time into building a production-ready version of Split! once school started it was a great design exercise for me that summer as I was just beginning my adventure into the Product Design major at Stanford. I learned a lot about how iterative of a process design is, some Objective-C and most importantly to always focus on the end user. I'm hoping to actually turn this design into a function app in Swift when I take CS193P (iPhone Application Programming) during Winter 2016.
